## The output at a glance

After a valuation run, you receive:

- **Estimated value per share** — the headline intrinsic value
- **Current market price** — for comparison
- **Implied upside/downside** — percentage difference between intrinsic value and market price
- **Full projection table** — 10-year revenue, EBIT, FCFF, and discount factors
- **Equity bridge** — how operating value becomes equity value per share
- **WACC breakdown** — cost of equity, cost of debt, and weights

## What to look for

### Is the growth rate reasonable?

Compare the projected revenue CAGR against the company's historical growth and the industry average. A 25% growth rate for a mature utility company should raise flags; the same rate for a high-growth SaaS company might be conservative.

### Do margins converge sensibly?

The model transitions current operating margins to a target margin over the projection period. Check that the target margin is realistic for the industry and that the convergence path makes sense.

### Is reinvestment consistent with growth?

The sales-to-capital ratio determines how much the company must reinvest to grow. Higher growth requires more reinvestment. If the model shows high growth with low reinvestment, the value may be overstated.

### Does the terminal value dominate?

In a well-calibrated DCF, the terminal value typically accounts for 50–70% of total firm value. If it's above 85%, the valuation is essentially a bet on what happens after year 10 — consider shortening the high-growth period or increasing near-term cash flows.

## What a DCF does NOT tell you

- **Timing** — a stock can be undervalued for years before the market agrees
- **Catalysts** — intrinsic value doesn't predict what will make the price move
- **Precision** — treat the output as a range, not a point estimate. Small changes in WACC or growth can shift the value by 20–30%

<Tip>
  Run the valuation twice with different assumptions (optimistic and pessimistic) to get a sense of the range. The difference between the two tells you how sensitive the valuation is.
</Tip>
